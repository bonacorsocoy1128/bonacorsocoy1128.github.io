It typically consists of the following stages:

1. Seed germination: The life cycle begins with the germination of a seed. Under appropriate environmental conditions, the seed starts to absorb water and nutrients, and the embryo within the seed starts to grow.

2. Seedling stage: After germination, the embryo develops into a young plant called a seedling. It produces roots that help in absorbing water and nutrients from the soil, and shoots that germinate into leaves for photosynthesis.

3. Vegetative growth: As the seedling grows, it undergoes vegetative growth, where its roots and shoots continue to develop. This stage is characterized by increased leaf production and the formation of branches or stems.

4. Flowering: When the plant reaches maturity, it enters the flowering stage. It produces flowers, which are the reproductive organs of the plant. Flowers can vary in size, shape, color, and scent, and their main function is to attract pollinators for fertilization.

5. Pollination: Pollination is the process by which pollen, containing male reproductive cells, is transferred from the male part of the flower (anther) to the female part (stigma) of the same or another flower. This enables the fertilization of the plant.

6. Fruiting: After successful pollination and fertilization, the female part of the flower develops into a fruit, which contains the seeds. The fruit helps in dispersing the seeds and protecting them until they are ready to germinate.

7. Seed dispersal: Once the seeds are mature, they are dispersed away from the parent plant to ensure the survival and colonization of new areas. Seeds can be dispersed by wind, water, animals, or other mechanisms.

8. Seed dormancy: When the seeds are dispersed, they may enter a period of dormancy, where they remain inactive until suitable conditions for germination are met. This allows the seeds to survive adverse environmental conditions and germinate when conditions are favorable.

The life cycle of a plant may vary depending on the species and environmental factors. Some plants may complete their life cycle within a few weeks or months, while others may take several years.
