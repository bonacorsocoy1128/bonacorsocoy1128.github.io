2. Securitization of mortgages: Mortgage lenders bundled their loans together and sold them as mortgage-backed securities (MBS) to other investors. 3. Irresponsible lending practices: Lenders were offering mortgages with low interest rates and lax lending standards, such as adjustable-rate mortgages (ARM) or no-documentation loans. When the housing market collapsed, these institutions suffered significant losses and some even faced insolvency.

5. Lack of regulatory oversight: Regulatory agencies and governments failed to effectively regulate the financial sector and prevent risky behavior. There was a lack of transparency and accountability, leading to the accumulation of systemic risks.

6. Global interconnectedness: The crisis rapidly spread across borders due to the interconnectedness of the global financial system. Financial institutions around the world held toxic assets and were intertwined through complex financial instruments, exacerbating the impact of the crisis.

Overall, the combination of these factors led to a severe credit crunch, a collapse in consumer spending and investment, and a deep recession in many countries. The crisis served as a wake-up call for policymakers and regulators to reassess their approach to risk management and strengthen oversight of the financial sector.
