1. Poverty: The majority of people who are hungry or malnourished live in poverty. They do not have sufficient income or resources to meet their basic needs, including access to food.

2. Conflict and Instability: Ongoing conflicts and instability in certain regions can disrupt food production, distribution, and access. This can lead to food shortages and increased hunger among affected populations.

3. Climate Change and Natural Disasters: Changing weather patterns, extreme weather events, and natural disasters such as droughts, floods, and hurricanes can contribute to crop failures and lower food production, causing hunger and malnutrition.

4. Limited Access to Agricultural Resources: Many developing countries lack access to modern agricultural technologies, such as improved seeds, fertilizers, and irrigation systems. This hinders their ability to produce sufficient food to meet the needs of their population.

5. Poor Infrastructure: Inadequate infrastructure, such as transportation systems and storage facilities, can lead to food waste and spoilage. It can also make it difficult to distribute food to remote or inaccessible areas.

6. Lack of Education and Knowledge: Insufficient knowledge about agriculture, nutrition, and food preparation can contribute to hunger and malnutrition. Lack of education and awareness about proper farming techniques, food storage, and nutritional requirements can limit food production and lead to unhealthy diets.

7. Unequal Distribution of Wealth and Resources: Inequitable distribution of land, wealth, and resources can result in limited access to productive land for farming, income disparity, and food insecurity.

8. Discrimination and Marginalization: Certain demographic groups, such as women, children, indigenous populations, and migrants, may face discrimination and marginalization, leading to higher rates of hunger and malnutrition.

9. Disease and Illness: Infectious diseases like HIV/AIDS, malaria, and tuberculosis can weaken individuals, making them more vulnerable to malnutrition and hunger.

10. Overpopulation: Rapid population growth in many parts of the world can put pressure on food supplies and lead to increased hunger if not managed properly.

It is important to address these underlying causes of hunger and starvation in order to effectively combat food insecurity and improve the lives of those affected.
